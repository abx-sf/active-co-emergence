Meaning as Interval

Part of the research sequence — Activation

Meaning is often treated as something that resides in content, as a latent property to be brought to light, or as something projected by the observer, a secondary effect of interpretation.

In both cases, it is stabilized too early. It is assumed to already exist, somewhere, and that the task is to recognize it or produce it. A submerged treasure hunt.

But what remains unseen is that meaning does not precede the relation that makes it possible. It is neither contained nor added. It happens.

It happens, however, under precise conditions. Not everywhere. Not always. Speaking of meaning as an event is not enough if the nature of this happening is not clarified. It is not an arbitrary appearance, nor a simple subjective activation.

Meaning emerges — takes form — when a relation holds between a structure and a configuration. It is a point of hold. Not an object. A temporary coherence.

For this reason, it is more accurate to think of it as an interval. Not a void between two terms, but a tension that makes the relation itself possible. As in music, it is not the single note that produces sense, but the relation between their positions within a structure. Likewise, meaning does not reside in elements, but in the relations that traverse them.

The interval is not neutral. It is constrained, oriented, made possible by a structure that defines its conditions. Not everything can happen. Meaning is what emerges when a tension holds without fully resolving.

This implies that content cannot be understood as a repository of meanings. Content does not contain. It organizes constraints. It is a field of already active relations, made of rhythms, implicit hierarchies, openings and resistances. It defines what cannot mean.

Some configurations are possible. Others are incompatible, not by choice or interpretation, but by structure. In this sense, content selects. Not by choosing, but by excluding.

Likewise, attention is not a neutral or empty device. It is already configured. It is traversed by expectations, habits, speeds, perceptual economy. It does not look. It anticipates.

It filters according to immediate compatibilities. It privileges what stabilizes quickly. It avoids what introduces friction. This selection is an operative condition. It allows orientation, but it reduces.

When this reduction dominates, the relation with content remains superficial. Not because it is false, but because it is not traversed. The meaning that emerges is immediate, readily available, recognizable, but fragile. It stabilizes before being tested.

It does not hold under variation. It does not reactivate when conditions change.

Meaning, then, does not arise from content or attention, but from their intersection. Not a superposition, but an operative compatibility between a structure and a configuration.

It is the point where the constraints of content and the posture of attention sustain a coherent relation. If this intersection does not occur, meaning does not emerge or collapses. If it does occur, what takes place is a temporary stabilization.

It becomes necessary, then, to distinguish between signification and meaning. Signification is the process. It is the dynamic through which relations are activated and articulated.

Meaning is a local effect of this process. It is a threshold at which something, for a moment, holds. It is neither origin nor end. It is a point of passage. It does not precede signification. It does not conclude it.

This stabilization is not guaranteed. Meaning can fail. It can emerge and dissolve immediately. Collapse is not an exception. It is an intrinsic possibility.

It occurs when the relation does not hold. It does not produce a void. It exposes the process. It makes visible that meaning is not given, but depends on conditions that may not hold.

In this perspective, even nonsense changes status. It is not the absence of meaning. It is a mode in which stabilization is suspended.

It can prevent premature closure. It can block automatic recognition. It can force attention out of its own economies. It does not destroy meaning. It pushes it to its limit.

If meaning is event and not content, the question of truth can no longer be posed in terms of direct correspondence. Meaning does not guarantee truth.

It can be coherent without being true. Its reliability depends on its capacity to hold in relation to the constraints of content and the relation that activates it.

Truth, if it still has a role, is at stake in differential persistence over time. In the capacity not to collapse immediately under variation.

To speak of meaning as a variable function is possible only by abandoning the idea of an autonomous function. Meaning has no fixed domain and no determined output. It depends on a dispositif.

It depends on the set of conditions that make a relation possible. It is within this system that it can emerge, stabilize, or collapse.

Meaning is not what something wants to say. It is not what is extracted. It is not what is possessed.

It is what happens when a relation manages to hold without fully closing. As in a musical interval, it is not in one note or the next, but in the tension that runs through them.

It is not preserved. It is not guaranteed. It is verified.

—

And it may not occur.

—

When it does not hold, nothing remains to be recovered. Not because something is missing, but because the relation did not hold.

—

What remains is not a meaning, but the possibility that it does not happen.
