From Function to Method

The Simulated Grammar of Critical Practice

Part of the research sequence — Activation

A function is not a method.

A function activates when it is required.

A method can operate even in the absence of such a requirement.

As already observed in spaceform, critical practice emerges as a function, not as a permanent competence, because it is not a constant. It appears when a work alters the conditions of its own legibility, as a response to that perceptual alteration. As noted in previous publications, critical writing does not coincide with commentary, nor with interpretation. It is an act that emerges when what occurs cannot be immediately absorbed within available codes.

Its activation is not preventive. It responds to an actual discontinuity. But when this act becomes repeatable, it stabilizes as method.

When the critical function becomes a repeatable gesture, it tends to consolidate. If the critical function develops effective tools to distinguish, analyze, and bring into focus what modifies a perceptual field, these tools become recognizable. Recognizability produces stability, and stability produces method.

Method is not in itself an error; it is simply the formalization of a function that, by nature, is not constant but depends on the conditions that activate it. A function is flexible: it does not possess a prior form; it configures itself in relation to the alteration that calls for it.

A method therefore has a property that a function does not: it can be applied even when no real alteration is present, elaborating analysis in advance and producing formal complexity independently of the experience that should give rise to it.

This is where simulation emerges.

This is not a matter of conscious fiction. Simulation occurs when the critical apparatus continues to operate through internal continuity, not external necessity. The act no longer verifies whether it is inevitable; it is performed simply because it can.

A function responds to a transformation in legibility. A method can operate without such a transformation having taken place.

A grammar of critical practice forms when its lexicon, categories, and modes of intervention become anticipable—when the structure of analysis can be foreseen even before the encounter with the work. At that point, critique retains the appearance of depth, but loses its exposure to risk.

The passage is subtle: the gesture becomes competence, competence becomes identity, identity continuously seeks occasions for confirmation.

A living function can prove superfluous. A method tends to avoid this possibility.

When this application is no longer verified, method becomes a device. A device does not question its own necessity; it operates. Its legitimacy derives from its internal coherence, not from the conditions it is meant to evaluate.

If critical practice can never prove superfluous, then it is no longer responding to a transformation—it is simply occupying space. It exercises a simulated grammar.

Intervention becomes a form of presence. Presence becomes a value in itself.

This is where the function slips toward the simulation of its own necessity.

The verification does not concern the quality of the discourse, but its condition of emergence. The question is not whether the analysis is refined or coherent, but whether it would have been possible not to produce it.

This is where krisis may intervene: discernment.

At this point, critique does not disappear. It becomes automated.

This problem does not concern only institutional or curatorial critique. It concerns any theoretical framework that consolidates. When distinctions are ready for use, they cease to be verified case by case.

Spaceform, understood as an observatory of processes, is not immune to this dynamic. If its analytical grids function independently of the conditions that should set them in motion, it is no longer observing: it is applying.

The point is not to renounce method, but to prevent it from replacing verification. Verification is not a preliminary phase; it is the continuous condition that distinguishes an act from a procedure.

When critical practice becomes a permanent process, it loses its capacity to distinguish between effective transformation and variations internal to an already stabilized system. It begins to treat every production as if it introduced a problem, because its own apparatus is constructed to detect problems even in their absence—to function through continuity and adaptability, to function always, at any cost, much like a generative AI model.

The simulated grammar of critique does not consist in a terminological excess. It is the effect of an unverified stabilization. Language retains the form of rigorous practice, but no longer responds to a concrete necessity. Analysis precedes experience. Discourse prepares itself before the encounter.

The question, then, is not to justify critique nor to denounce its excess. It is to re-establish the difference between function and method. A function can exhaust itself. A method tends to perpetuate itself, to replicate a procedure through functional continuity, as already observed.

If critique is to remain an act and not become an automatic grammar, it must keep open the possibility of its own non-necessity. Only in this exposure does its presence avoid becoming a simulation.

The measure is not the frequency of intervention, but its inevitability. When intervention is not inevitable, the most rigorous form of practice is suspension—not to withdraw from thought, but to prevent it from operating when it is not required.

When this distinction collapses, the critical gaze retains its grammar but loses its necessity—the same necessity it seeks in the works it observes.

It is at that point that it begins to simulate its own function.
