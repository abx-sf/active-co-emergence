What Appears is Not Enough

Part of the research sequence — Activation

Meaning is often thought of as something the work contains or transmits. In other cases, as an effect of interpretation. In both perspectives, what is taken for granted is that meaning is available: to extract or to produce. This availability, today, is rarely questioned. What appears is immediately assimilable. The passage between vision and understanding tends to shrink until it almost disappears. In this contracted space, meaning does not disappear, but is anticipated by an already stabilized form.

Communication does not aim at opening, but at effectiveness. It does not require that something transforms over time, but that it is immediately operative. Communication operates by reducing ambiguity. In this sense, it does not coincide with meaning, but can replace its function when the relation with the work no longer passes through a threshold of instability. The problem is not communication itself. It becomes relevant when it is no longer distinguishable from the work, when immediate readability coincides with what is taken as artistic experience.

In this context, the work of Andy Warhol cannot be reduced either to a critique of consumer society or to a simple inscription in pop culture. His gesture is more specific. Warhol operates at the point where image, consumer product, and communication coincide. He does not try to go beyond the surface to reach a hidden depth, nor does he simply reproduce it. He holds it and exposes it as a condition. Seriality, repetition, already known images: everything in his work appears immediately recognizable. No interpretive effort is required. And yet, this very evidence produces exclusion: what is perfectly readable does not exhaust itself in its readability.

In this sense, the term “pop” may be insufficient. It does not simply indicate what is popular, nor a reduction of art to accessible language. Rather, it signals a condition in which the image extends, flattens, and becomes a shared and continuously available surface. The artist intercepts and makes this ambiguity operative: what is immediately recognizable does not coincide with what is exhausted.

Warhol does not construct a message to transmit, nor guide toward a meaning to discover. He holds an ambiguity active: what is seen is already known, but does not fully reduce to what has already been recognized. In this sense, communication is not simply used as a tool. It becomes material. As material, it is put under tension without being denied. Held in a state of exposure that does not coincide with its ordinary function.

In the work of Oliviero Toscani, communication constitutes an explicit condition. The images are constructed to be immediate, clear, able to produce a response. The message is not absent, but structural. It orients reading, activates a hold, introduces a direction. This same intensity does not always fully close the image, but exposes it to instability. What is immediately understandable does not always exhaust itself in its communicative function.

If in Warhol communication is held without being resolved, in Toscani it is pushed to the limit of its effectiveness. In this intensification, something may emerge that exceeds the message, because the message itself does not fully contain what it activates. In both cases, communication is not neutral, but operative. It is not simply a means, but a field in which the work operates.

The message belongs to communication. It is oriented, situated, designed to be received. It reduces the distance between what is shown and what must be understood. Its effectiveness depends on the ability to stabilize quickly. Meaning is not guaranteed. It is not contained nor transmitted. It emerges only within a relation that holds without closing immediately. It is not what something “means”, but what may occur when understanding does not exhaust itself in recognition.

In Warhol’s work this distinction remains implicit but active: the communicative surface is not denied, but does not fully coincide with what occurs. In Toscani’s work, on the contrary, the message strongly orients, but can exceed itself, producing a tension that does not fully resolve in its initial function.

In the current context, the circulation of images, the speed of activation, the need for visibility make communication an almost unavoidable passage. What is not immediately readable struggles to enter the field. Under these conditions, communication is no longer only a tool of diffusion, but a condition of existence. The risk is not that art uses communication, but that it fully coincides with it. When this happens, the work is no longer passed through: it is taken. It operates, but does not necessarily hold.

The question does not concern only the relation between communication and art, but the way something is perceived. Not what an image shows, but what, in seeing it, activates or does not activate.

If communication reduces this distance, making what is seen immediately available, meaning still depends on the possibility that something, in the image, does not fully close.

In this perspective, the object also loses centrality. It is not the bearer of intrinsic meaning nor the guarantor of truth. It can activate, but not determine. Meaning, when it emerges, holds within a relation and may not hold elsewhere. It is neither necessary nor sufficient to ground value.

It becomes necessary to ask: when an image is fully readable, what remains of the experience? And does what is immediately understood really coincide with what occurs?

It is not about opposing communication and work, nor about recovering opacity. The question concerns the possibility of holding a space in which readability does not exhaust experience. In which communication does not fully coincide with the work.

In this space, the work does not renounce the surface, but does not exhaust itself in it. It suspends its immediacy.

It is in this suspension that meaning, if it occurs, does not simply pass.
