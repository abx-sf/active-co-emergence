Active Co-Emergence — The Language in the Time of Attention

spaceform — Critical Research Space

Conditions of an Absent Practice

Part of the research sequence — Activation

Critique is not a discourse about the work. It is a practice of temporal traversal of the work.

Critique does not disappear when it ceases to be practiced. It disappears when it is no longer necessary. Not because the world produces fewer objects worthy of attention, but because attention itself is reconfigured as passage, not as dwelling.

Within this reconfiguration, the critical gesture loses status not through censorship or removal, but through incompatibility. It is no longer required. It is no longer anticipated.

Critique, in its original sense, is not a discourse about the object. It is an interruption in time. An act that holds what appears, separates it from the flow, exposes it to a duration that does not coincide with the instant of perception.

To critique means to suspend evidence, to defer consensus, to accept that sense is not immediately available. Without this suspension, there is no knowledge, only recognition.

When critique does not operate, objects continue to exist. They appear, circulate, are exchanged, shared. But they do not insist. They do not return. They do not accumulate differences.

Their presence does not become experience, but succession. What is missing is not meaning, but the possibility that meaning stabilizes over time.

In the absence of critique, the world does not become mute: it becomes indifferent. When critique is deactivated, it is not only art that loses thickness; it is the entire system of sense production that flattens.

This absence is not a void. It is a saturation. Attention is continuously solicited, but never held. Everything is immediately accessible and therefore immediately substitutable.

Where everything is available, nothing is necessary. Critique, which requires choice, loss, renunciation, thus finds itself deprived of its operational space. It is not rejected: it is rendered superfluous.

The critical gesture implies a form of responsibility that is not moral, but temporal. To decide to stop at something means subtracting time from something else.

Thought, to be critical, must be able to fail. It must be able to remain alone. It must be able to be contested. When these conditions are no longer sustainable, critique transforms into language without arrest.

Art is not the first to lose this possibility. Art is only one of the places where the loss becomes visible.

Without critique, the work is not denied. It is consumed as event. It does not enter a history of thought, but a sequence of appearances.

Critique does not originate in art. It originates as a human device of discernment in time.

The loss of critique is therefore not the loss of a profession or a role. It is the loss of a cognitive function.

Today critique is adrift because it has lost its methodological self-legitimation. It no longer knows what it can demand, where to stop, when to say no.

To speak of conditions of an absent practice means recognizing that absence is not accidental. It is the coherent outcome of a configuration of attention that privileges movement over duration.

Where someone accepts to stop, to return, to sustain a difference over time, critique reemerges. Not as institution, but as possibility. Not as answer, but as a question that insists.

Krisis is: to decide, to separate, to hold, to assume consequences.

— Abraxas
