The Automatic Atmosphere

Critique and Technology in the Age of Generative Language

Part of the research sequence — Activation

The proliferation of texts on art is not a sign of vitality.

It is a sign of stabilization.

When language becomes easily generable, something changes in its function.

Not everything that can be written still needs to be thought.

Artificial intelligence does not introduce this problem.

It makes it visible.

Every technology that enters the symbolic space first produces inflation. Not because it increases the capacity to think, but because it multiplies the possibility of imitating thought.

It happened with the printing press.

It happened with photography.

It happened with cinema.

And it is happening today with artificial intelligence.

It is necessary to wait for this inflation to subside, to practice an economy of thought, and to restore conceptual values so that, once the crisis has passed, meaning remains to tell the time that has been lived through.

When printing spread across Europe in the fifteenth century, the problem was not the lack of books but their excess. Reproduction multiplied texts and, along with them, everything that had previously remained marginal: compilations, commentaries, repetitions. The existence of a text no longer guaranteed its value.

Yet at the same moment something new emerged: the need to distinguish, to select, and to take a position.

This is where modern critique begins to take shape.

Photography produced a similar phenomenon. When the image became technically reproducible, the visual world expanded until it saturated the space of experience. Not everything that could be photographed became meaningful. On the contrary, proliferation made a more radical difference visible: the one between image and gaze.

This transition was observed by Walter Benjamin, who argued that technology does not simply destroy previous forms but forces them to redefine their meaning.

Every technology that multiplies the conditions of production initially generates an undifferentiated proliferation. Only later do new forms of selection emerge.

Generative artificial intelligence belongs to this same genealogy.

Its most immediate effect is not the replacement of thought, but the multiplication of language. Descriptions, articles, critical texts, and presentations can now be generated and reformulated almost instantly.

The result is not the disappearance of discourse on art, but its expansion.

Critique becomes an environment.

Increasingly, language does not intervene in artworks. The work becomes immersed in a discursive surface that makes it immediately compatible with the system that hosts it.

This phenomenon did not originate with artificial intelligence. In its modern origins, critique had a different function.

In the eighteenth century, Denis Diderot observed the exhibitions of the Salon and wrote in his Salon texts (1759–1781) something that had not truly existed before: a public reflection on artworks. Not a neutral description, but a direct confrontation with what was being shown.

In Diderot’s texts, critical writing intervenes in the works and introduces judgment.

Critique therefore did not arise as an accompaniment to art. It emerged as a position.

A century later, Charles Baudelaire radicalized this gesture and, in his Salon writings (1845–1859), openly attacked many works celebrated by the artistic system of his time. He defended marginal painters, polemicized with the academy, defined photography as “the servant of the sciences and the arts,” and expressed his judgment without protection.

But every technology modifies the boundaries within which this responsibility is exercised.

When photography appeared, many believed that painting would become useless. The machine seemed capable of replacing the hand and human creativity. We now know that the opposite occurred, precisely because photography radically transformed the conditions of representation: painting changed direction. It no longer had to compete with the faithful representation of reality and was finally freed from the constraint of the real itself, opening new possibilities.

Here Benjamin’s lesson returns: technology, techno-logos, forces the artist, the intellectual, and the thinker to redefine the meaning of their work and of thought itself.

From that moment on, the problem of representing reality gradually shifted, in part, toward photography.

With the progressive institutionalization of the art system, however, this function changed over time. An increasing portion of critical texts no longer emerged as interventions but as accompaniment. Language became mediation between artworks, institutions, and the public. Within this process a recognizable language gradually emerged: fluid, evocative, rarely conflictual. A language that describes more than it distinguishes.

Here a discursive automatism appears: critical practice becomes an environment.

Not because critique is inherently automatic, but because part of its language becomes replicable. Rhetorical structures stabilize, metaphors repeat themselves, and descriptions assume interchangeable forms.

The result is a critical gaze without risk.

When generative models begin to produce critical texts, something revealing occurs. They do not invent a new language. They replicate the one that already exists. Their capacity to produce texts rapidly and continuously is more than a technological novelty. It introduces a new condition: language becomes easily generable.

When language becomes easily generable, it ceases to be sufficient proof of thought.

This does not mean that language loses importance. It means that its status changes.

In this sense, artificial intelligence does not introduce automatism into critical language. It makes it visible.

Artificial intelligence learns from the dominant patterns of cultural discourse. If that discourse has become predictable, its imitation becomes surprisingly easy.

Yet every inflation also produces an unexpected effect.

Quantity no longer guarantees relevance.Attention shifts elsewhere: to the structure of thought and to the position a text assumes with respect to the field it describes.

The critical function does not disappear. It transforms once more.

It can no longer limit itself to producing descriptions or interpretive atmospheres. These operations can now be delegated and replicated almost instantly by a generative artificial intelligence model. What becomes decisive again is the capacity to intervene in the form of the space in which works and discourses emerge.

The inflation of language—both critical and visual—therefore does not eliminate the critical exercise; it likely liberates it, forcing it to rediscover its original function: to distinguish and to introduce judgment — krisis.

Artificial intelligence does not threaten critique.

It threatens the automatic language that had already been imitating it.

AI can produce critical texts that appear critical, but are not truly so. They are another form of critique without risk.

In this sense, artificial intelligence does not represent an end.

It is a revelation.

A social mirror.

It reveals a crisis that was already underway in the language of critical practice and in that of images.
